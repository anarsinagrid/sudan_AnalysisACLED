What Changed in Sudan After April 2023?

A popular movement interrupted — and how violence reorganized a country

Sudan, a country anchored by the Nile and centuries of cultural exchange — from the Nubian kingdoms of Napata and Meroe to Khartoum’s modern music and literary scenes — with economic fundamentals in irrigated agriculture, livestock, gum arabic, gold, and regional trade. In another trajectory, the civic energy of the pre-war years could have gone into building institutions, services, and cultural life rather than survival and displacement. Before that collapse, Sudan also held space for cultural and civic achievement: the 2019–2022 protest movement itself showed coordinated public action across cities, documented in open sources such as the record of the 2019–2022 protests.

This analysis asks a narrow but important question: what structurally changed in Sudan after April 15, 2023? Rather than treating the war as a sudden escalation of an already violent situation, the goal is to show how the form of political activity, the actors involved, the geography of harm, and the visibility of death itself were reorganized.

The figures that follow are not intended to measure suffering precisely. They are intended to show how violence became the dominant system through which power, survival, and control were exercised.

Questions We Test
  • Was April 15, 2023 a temporary shock — or a structural break in how violence is recorded and experienced?
  • When protest disappears, what replaces it in the event record: battles, strategic violence, civilian targeting?
  • Do SAF and RSF behave like comparable forces, or do they produce distinct patterns of harm?
  • Does violence spread evenly, or concentrate in a few regions — and what can "quiet" areas in the data actually mean?

ACT I — Hope

Before the War, There Was a Movement

Long before April 2023, Sudan's streets were already political. Ordinary citizens gathered again and again, not with weapons, but with chants, signs, and a growing belief that collective pressure could reshape power.

Protest was not an exception to daily life. It was becoming part of it.

These were not isolated eruptions of anger. They were sustained, repeated, and spread across cities — slowly building confidence and coordination.

The data from this period does not simply show more events. It shows structure.

Sudan was not drifting toward chaos. It was actively experimenting with civic pressure.

What matters about this period is not simply that protests occurred, but that they were repeated, coordinated, and sustained. Event data from this phase shows regular civic activity across multiple locations, indicating that protest had become a routine political tool, not an exceptional outburst.

This matters because it establishes a baseline: political participation existed and was observable. The disappearance of protests later is therefore not a mystery to be explained away, but a phenomenon that requires explanation.

ACT II — Tension

Power Was Watching

As protest activity stabilized, tension moved elsewhere. Away from the streets and into military bases, negotiation rooms, and command structures.

Violence had not yet displaced protest, but it increasingly surrounded it.

Armed actors were present. Negotiations stalled. The country was still moving — but toward a narrowing set of outcomes.

April 15, 2023

On April 15, the logic of political participation collapsed.

Armed conflict erupted between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces, transforming cities into battlefields and civilians into immediate targets.

From this moment on, survival replaced mobilization as the dominant concern.

The days around April 15 should not be understood as a gradual slide. They represent a discrete collapse of one political logic and the sudden activation of another.

Once armed conflict began between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF), the costs of public political presence increased dramatically. From this point forward, participation in collective action carried risks comparable to being in a battle zone.

This transition is what the remaining figures aim to document.

The humanitarian implications were immediate and regionwide (UNHCR crisis overview).

ACT III — Rupture

When Violence Became the System

The most visible consequence of the war was not a single spike in deaths, but the establishment of a new and sustained level of lethality.

After April 15, fatalities rose sharply and remained far above anything observed before. This was not a temporary surge — it was a structural shift.

April 15, 2023 marks the start of open warfare between the SAF and RSF. The immediate spike that follows captures urban combat, shelling, and civilians suddenly exposed inside battle zones — not a single incident, but a rapid change in how and where fighting occurred.

How to read this: Look for the step change after April 15 rather than a single peak. The apparent mid‑2024 dip should not be read as peace — indirect deaths and reporting lags suppress counts. The return of steeper lines in late 2024–2025 signals renewed urban offensives.

The apparent decline in early–mid 2024 does not mean violence eased. As frontlines stalled, civilians died from siege, starvation, disease, and displacement — harms that are systematically underreported in event data and therefore depress the visible curve. (See famine context: 2024–present famine).

By late 2024 and into 2025, RSF–SAF offensives resumed, including contestation of major cities such as Al Fasher (Siege of El Fasher). Urban re-entry and artillery use drove documented fatalities back up, even as indirect deaths continued outside the narrow scope of battle reports.

The timeline also reveals quieter phases that are easy to misread. In early 2024, reported fatalities appear to decline.

This does not reflect peace. It reflects stalemate.

As frontlines froze, mortality increasingly came from starvation, disease, and siege conditions — deaths that are slower and harder to document.

By late 2024, large-scale offensives returned, bringing renewed intensity to Khartoum, Gezira, and Darfur.

This figure shows monthly aggregated fatalities from ACLED, which is crucial for interpretation. Monthly aggregation smooths day-to-day volatility and highlights structural changes, not tactical noise.

Three analytically distinct phases are visible:

  1. April 2023: Structural break, not a spike. The sharp rise beginning in April 2023 reflects the onset of open warfare. Urban fighting, artillery use, and the collapse of civilian–military separation exposed large populations to direct harm. What matters is not the height of the first peak, but the fact that fatalities never return to pre-April levels.
  2. Early–mid 2024: Apparent decline. The visible dip in 2024 is easy to misread. This is not a peace dividend. Instead, frontlines hardened, sieges expanded, and harm shifted toward starvation, disease, and displacement — forms of death that are systematically under-captured in event datasets like ACLED. In other words, the curve drops because the violence becomes less event-like, not because it becomes less deadly.
  3. Late 2024–2025: Renewed increase. As contested cities such as Al Fasher re-enter active battle phases, fatalities rise again. Urban re-entry produces documented deaths because violence becomes more visible, more reportable, and more concentrated.

Key takeaway: This graph should be read as a record of how violence is expressed and documented, not as a complete ledger of human loss.

How Political Expression Was Replaced

War does not only increase violence. It changes what kinds of actions are even possible.

The disappearance of protests here should not be read as public disengagement. It reflects that mass gatherings became physically unsafe and were reclassified as clashes or were prevented entirely, shifting observable activity into armed categories.

When reading the next figure, focus on which event types occupy the space once held by protest, and avoid assuming that fewer protests imply acquiescence — the opportunity structure, not the grievances, changed.

Who Fought — and How

Although often described as a single conflict, the war is shaped by two very different patterns of violence.

SAF operations concentrate on conventional battles and territorial control. RSF actions more often target civilians or use coercive tactics away from formal frontlines, producing different harm profiles even when headline event counts look similar.

Read the actor charts as complementary: overlapping spikes do not mean identical behavior. Pay attention to where each actor’s activity sits on the spectrum from combat to civilian targeting.

External trackers underscore this asymmetry and its stakes (CFR conflict tracker).

ACT IV — Unfinished

A Movement Interrupted, Not Erased

April 15, 2023 did not mark the beginning of violence in Sudan. It marked the collapse of civic space under sustained armed conflict.

This is not an aftermath in the usual sense. The war has continued beyond the window shown in this report, and so has the suffering.

The analysis stops where the dataset stops: the latest event date in the data used here is January 23, 2025. What comes after is not an "after" in reality - it is beyond the boundary of observation.

Protest did not disappear because people stopped caring. It disappeared because war made collective presence impossible.

The war did not erase political demand in Sudan. It erased the conditions under which political participation could safely occur. The data shows a transition from organized civic pressure to enforced silence, mediated through violence. Understanding this shift is essential for interpreting both the conflict and any future attempts at political reconstruction.

Whatever one's beliefs, the hope is the same: protection of civilians, access for aid, and a durable political settlement.
